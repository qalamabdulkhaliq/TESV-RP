# Frostfall Roleplay — Server Lore

*Short lore pieces for Discord and the in-game courier board. Written for players who want the texture without the wiki. Each one stands alone.*

---

## The White-Gold Concordat: A Betrayal of Skyrim

The Great War ended in **4E 175.** The Empire had held. Barely.

The terms of the White-Gold Concordat were simple and devastating. The Aldmeri Dominion walked away with territory in Hammerfell, and there were riots in Dragonstar because of it. The Empire agreed to ban the worship of Talos across all its provinces, and there were rumors and speeches of defiance across each one. And in Skyrim, the office of **High Kingship** that had stood since the First Era was dissolved. The Jarls now answer to Imperial authority directly, with no native head of state to buffer them.

The year is **4E 191.** The Thalmor, ever at the throat of the rest of Tamriel, have grown restless.

Most Nords will tell you they were betrayed by an Empire they'd witnessed unspeakable horrors in the name of. Some never came back to tell anyone at all. The Empire founded by the very same god that had headed its pantheon of the Nine, made it a crime punishable by hanging to worship him. The soldiers who bled at the Red Ring, those that had watched comrades blown to gelly by elven thunderbolts, came home to find their god illegal and their king abolished by the stroke of a pen. The Empire will tell you it had no choice. That without the Concordat, there would be no Empire.

And the Empire would tell you the truth. The war is over. The ink is **sixteen years** dry.
But the argument, however, is not.

## Ulfric Stormcloak: The Man, the Prisoner, the Question

Ask ten Nords about Ulfric Stormcloak and you will get ten different answers.

Some say he is the last true voice of Skyrim, a man who refused to let the Concordat stand unopposed, who trained his voice like a weapon and his conviction like armor. They say he speaks for every Nord who ever bowed to an elf with a warrant and gritted their teeth.

Some say he is a fool. That the rebellion he was building would have gotten thousands of people killed for a war nobody could win, not yet, not with the Dominion at full strength and the Empire still licking its wounds.

Some say he is a prisoner in an Imperial cell waiting for a fate nobody will name out loud, and that the question of what happens to him is the only thing keeping Windhelm quiet.

Nobody agrees on what he is. Everyone agrees on what his absence means. The Stormcloak movement has a symbol. It does not have a general. It does not have a plan. It has people who are waiting, watching, and getting angrier by the day.

## Ulfric and High King Torygg

Most people who hated Ulfric Stormcloak assumed he hated the High King.

He did not.

By his own account, Ulfric saw Torygg as a young man handed a broken inheritance. A king whose throne rested on an agreement that humiliated his people. Ulfric believed the challenge he intended to issue was not an attack on a man, but a call to a king. Rise. Lead. Refuse the Concordat. Show Skyrim that its ruler would rather fall with honor than govern under occupation.

Torygg never got the chance to answer.

The High King was dead before the war Ulfric wanted had a place to begin. What Ulfric called a duel became a killing in the eyes of half the province. The man who wanted to start a righteous war is now in a cell, charged with murder, waiting to find out if the Empire agrees.

History does not care about intent. It records what happened.

## The Imperial Garrison: Veterans of a War They Feel They Lost

The soldiers garrisoning Skyrim in 4E 191 are not the Fourth Legion. That comes later, with Tullius, with fresh orders and a cleaner mandate.

These are men and women who have been in Skyrim for years. Some of them fought in the Great War. All of them came home to a province that does not entirely want them here, enforcing a Concordat that most of them find as distasteful as the Nords do.

They man the forts between holds. They escort East Empire caravans. They hunt bandits and break up the occasional brawl. They do not enforce the Talos ban. Not actively. They look the other way, and the Thalmor know it, and nobody says anything about it because the alternative is a confrontation that nobody is ready for.

They are tired soldiers doing a job in a province that has never fully accepted them, under orders that were handed down by a treaty they did not sign, for an Empire they still believe in despite everything.

Ask them why they stay. Most of them will shrug. Some of them will tell you the truth: because if they leave, something worse moves in.

## The Thalmor: What They Actually Want

The easy answer is Talos. Hunt the worshippers, burn the shrines, enforce the Concordat.

That is the job. It is not the goal.

The Aldmeri Dominion's long ambition is the restoration of Elvish supremacy across Tamriel. The Thalmor who study history, the ones with patience and a long view, believe that the Empire is a structure that has already failed. It survived the Great War. It will not survive the next one. Their work is to ensure that when the Empire finally collapses, the Dominion is positioned to fill the space it leaves behind.

The Talos ban is useful. Every shrine they burn is a provocation. Every arrest reminds the Nords that their god is illegal and the Empire agreed to make him so. A province full of people who hate the Empire is a province that will not rally to defend it.

They are not in Skyrim to win a religious argument. They are in Skyrim to make the eventual war easier to fight.

They are patient. They are few, for now. They have a plan that spans decades.

Watch them carefully. Not because of what they do today. Because of what today is building toward.

## The College of Winterhold: Magic in a Province That Fears It

The College has always been apart from Skyrim. Geographically, perched on a cliff above a dying town. Politically, never fully under any Jarl's thumb. Philosophically, committed to the pursuit of knowledge over the approval of people who would rather not think too hard about where magic comes from.

Under the White-Gold Concordat, the question of magical freedom is newly complicated. The Thalmor have canonical authority in Skyrim. The Aldmeri Dominion has a particular interest in controlling who practices magic, who studies it, and what they do with it. A Justiciar has already appeared in Winterhold. No action was taken. The visit was noted.

The College has not acknowledged the visit publicly.

The scholars inside argue about what to do. Some believe the College's independence is worth defending at any cost. Some believe that independence requires compromise to survive. Some are watching the Thalmor and trying to figure out what they actually want from an institution full of people who can burn down a building with a word.

Magic in Skyrim has never been popular. Right now, it has never been more politically inconvenient. Whether the College survives the next decade with its independence intact is a question that has not been answered yet.

## The Eight Divines: Faith in a Province That Lost One of Its Gods

The Nords have always worshipped the Nine Divines. Eight of them are openly recognized across the Empire. The ninth is Talos. Tiber Septim. The man who united Tamriel and was raised to godhood upon his death.

He is now illegal.

The White-Gold Concordat stripped Talos from the official pantheon. The Aldmeri Dominion denies his divinity entirely. To them, he was a man, a conqueror, a human whose worshippers are engaged in dangerous theological delusion.

To a Nord, he is the god who proved that men could rise above their station. That ambition and will could reach further than anyone thought possible. Talos is not just a deity to them. He is a statement about what men are capable of.

The temples of the Eight Divines remain open. The priests speak carefully. The worshippers who come to kneel at the empty space where a ninth shrine used to stand say nothing.

The Thalmor are watching. The Imperial garrison is not. Everybody is pretending the situation is manageable.

In Windhelm, there is a Temple of Talos that has not officially closed. What that means for the city, for its Jarl, for the Thalmor who have noticed, is a question that Skyrim will have to answer before long.
